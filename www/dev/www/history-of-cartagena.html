Cartagena, Colombia: Looking Back at Noteworthy Events in its Past

A port city on Colombia's Caribbean coast places two remarkable parts of its history --- its past and present. Cartagena has two contrasting neighborhoods: the ultramodern developed hotel-filled Bocagrande and the Old Walled City. These two popular places in Cartagena depict the progress it has gone through different eras.

Run through these noteworthy events in the past of Cartagena and relate how it has shaped its present-day.

  • Before the arrival of the Spanish, there were inhabitants of different indigenous groups in Cartagena and later abandoned the place.
  • Gold had been found in the tombs of indigenous leaders. Attracting many people to move to Cartagena, making the area rapidly grow from a village to a city. These people had ill motives; mainly, to rob the city’s treasure and riches.
  • The city was founded on the 1st of June 1533, by the Spanish Commander Pedro de Heredia from the former location of the indigenous Caribbean Calamarí village. Most of Heredia’s sailors had resided in Cartagena, Spain; hence, the new-found city got its name.
  • Despite the tragic events that happened, Cartagena irresistibly emerged as an important commercial port. The defining characteristic of Cartagena's colonial history is the conversion of the city into a preeminent port for all of Spain's northern South American colonies. Considering its ideal geographical position, the city has become a major trading center.
  • The prosperity of the city and the riches that passed through it made it the target of attacks by pirates and other European empires. The city was robbed several times with a hefty amount of gold.
  • It was Francis Drake's siege, an English sea captain and slave trader, that prompted the construction of Cartagena's city walls, which are emblematic of the city today.

Walled City Cartagena: The Strong Wall It Has Become Today

Cartagena was one of the first towns to proclaim independence from Spain. The desire for independence was ignited by an uprising in Bogota in 1810 and on November 11, 1811, Cartagena officially declared its independence from Spain.

Cartagena's independence is annually commemorated on November 11. While this celebration is focused on the city of Cartagena, this is a national holiday as the independence of Cartagena is seen as a key event in the struggle for freedom in entire Colombia and other parts of Latin America.

Despite all the changes, the 'walled' Old City remains unchanged. It continued to prosper through the export of oil, platinum, coffee, sugar, tobacco and textiles.

The long history of Cartagena has left a number of historical treasures which made the city a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1984. Along with its year-round warm weather and Caribbean beaches, the number of tourists who travel to Cartagena has increased.

Today, Cartagena has rapidly expanded and is now home to over 1 million inhabitants and is still the country's largest port and source of petrochemicals.
